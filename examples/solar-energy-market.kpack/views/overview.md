# Solar Energy Market — Overview

## Cost Thesis

Utility-scale solar PV is now the cheapest source of new electricity in most global markets. The cost decline is structural: a 22% learning curve (cost reduction per capacity doubling) has held for four decades, driven by manufacturing scale rather than subsidies. Grid parity has been achieved in approximately 75% of markets, and subsidy removal in mature economies has not reversed adoption trends.

A minority view holds that silicon material costs impose a theoretical floor, meaning further LCOE declines will slow. This tension is informative rather than contradictory — it reveals a timeline question, not a directional disagreement.

## Technology Landscape

**Bifacial modules** now account for 70% of new installations globally, with rear-side generation gains of 5-25% depending on ground reflectivity. Monofacial is declining to niche applications. Bankability is established.

**Perovskite tandem cells** represent the leading next-generation technology. Lab efficiency has reached 33.7% (Oxford PV, certified by Fraunhofer ISE). The lab-to-fab gap remains significant: commercial viability at utility scale is estimated for 2028-2030. The hard gate is durability — moisture and heat degradation pathways are known but not fully mitigated, and the industry requires 25-year warranty coverage that no perovskite product can yet demonstrate.

## Systemic Risk

The highest systemic risk is supply chain concentration. China controls over 80% of polysilicon production and 95% of wafer manufacturing. A tariff or geopolitical disruption could create 12-18 month supply gaps. This is not a technology problem — it is a trade policy and industrial diversification problem. 8 out of 10 independent analyses flagged this as the top risk.
