# Letter to the Consignor — Decline Pending Revised Terms

> Formal-register decline letter for the consignor. Sent under the
> committee chair's signature on letterhead.

---

Dear Vendor V,

Thank you for the opportunity to review the bronze sculpture for inclusion in our forthcoming sale of nineteenth-century European works. We have completed our examination, including a review of the foundry mark and consultation of the relevant published references.

It is our conclusion, on the strength of the foundry mark on the underside, that the work was cast at Foundry F, Paris, during the period 1925–1940. As Foundry F began operating in 1925 — after Sculptor Y's death in 1913 — the work is a posthumous cast after Sculptor Y, rather than a lifetime work.

We accept that the 1971 estate-sale catalog you have provided describes the work simply as "by Sculptor Y." That description was correct under the conventions of its time; it does not, however, distinguish lifetime from posthumous casts in the way contemporary practice requires. We do not believe the 1971 catalog was in error; we believe its language has, since the 1980s, ceased to be sufficient on its own.

A posthumous cast by Foundry F is a saleable category. Three comparable Foundry F casts after Sculptor Y achieved hammer prices between €80,000 and €140,000 at auction in 2024 and 2025. We would be glad to include the work in an upcoming sale on this basis.

The terms under which we can accept the consignment are the following:

1. The catalog attribution would read "after Sculptor Y, posthumous cast, Foundry F, c. 1925–1940."
2. The reserve would be revised to align with the posthumous-cast market range — between €80,000 and €140,000, to be agreed in the revised consignment agreement.
3. The revised consignment agreement would supersede the draft of 2026-04-22.

If these terms are not workable, we will return the work and the supporting documentation to you with our thanks for the consideration.

We understand that this conclusion may be different from the one reflected in the 1971 catalog and from the family understanding of the work. We hope it is useful that the foundry-mark evidence is dispositive of the cast period; the question of lifetime authorship is not one on which our specialists have discretion.

Please let us know how you would like to proceed.

Yours sincerely,

The Committee
